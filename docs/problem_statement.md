# Problem Statement — India: Stop Electricity Theft, Recover Revenue, Restore Reliability

Problem (one line):
India’s distribution utilities are losing billions every year to electricity theft and distribution losses — undermining reliability, raising tariffs, and starving modernization.

Scale & statistics:
- Aggregate Technical & Commercial (AT&C) losses across India are commonly reported in the mid‑teens (≈15–20%) at the national level; in worst‑affected states and feeders losses can exceed 30–40%.
- In high‑loss pockets, industry and state filings indicate that theft, meter tampering, and unmetered consumption account for a very large share of commercial losses (commonly estimated at ~40–60% of commercial loss in these feeders).
- Conservatively, these losses translate into recurring multi‑billion USD shortfalls (tens of thousands of crores INR) every year — a structural drain on DISCOM cash flow and an immediate fiscal problem.

Immediate impacts:
- Utilities: persistent revenue gaps, higher borrowing, deferred maintenance, and delayed investment in meters and IT.
- Consumers: tariff pressure and more frequent outages; vulnerable households face higher risk during heatwaves and peak demand.
- System: increased emergency purchases, grid stress, and delayed integration of renewables and EV charging.

Technical challenge (precise, operational):
- Detect anomalous and fraudulent meter/feeder behavior in near‑real time across heterogeneous fleets of legacy and smart meters.
- Localize likely theft to feeders or clusters so field teams can inspect and remediate with high ROI.
- Prioritize alerts by expected recovered revenue and enforcement cost to maximize the impact of limited inspection budgets.
- Operate reliably under Indian field constraints: intermittent telemetry, mixed communications, legacy meters, and noisy data.

Why existing approaches fail:
- Periodic audits, blanket replacements, and simple thresholding are too slow, too expensive, or produce too many false positives; they miss stealthy and localized theft that cumulatively causes the most damage.

Measurable success criteria (judge‑ready):
- Absolute AT&C reduction on pilot feeders (percentage points) within 3–12 months.
- Net recovered INR per inspection and cost‑per‑recovered‑INR demonstrating positive ROI.
- Reduction in emergency procurement spend and a measurable drop in outages on remediated feeders.
- Field conversion rate: percentage of alerts that lead to validated recovery within a defined window.

Judging rubric (what to evaluate):
- Pre/post measurement methodology and clean baseline data.
- Field‑verified evidence (meter events, inspection logs, photos) that link alerts to remediation.
- Economic analysis: cost, recovered value, and time‑to‑ROI.
- A realistic scale‑up and operational plan that accounts for DISCOM constraints.

Why judges must act now:
Rapidly rising demand (cooling, EVs) and constrained utility finances make this a time‑sensitive crisis: every month without targeted detection increases cumulative losses and delays critical grid upgrades. A working, deployable solution yields immediate, high‑impact financial and reliability gains — not theoretical benefits years from now.

Where Helios fits:

Evidence to include in submissions:
- Pilot pre/post AT&C figures and recovered revenue with methodology.
- Inspection logs and auditable remediation evidence.
- Cost‑per‑inspection and projected economics for scaling.
- A short operational playbook showing how alerts converted into field action and verified recovery.

This is urgent, measurable, and solvable. Judges should prioritize solutions that demonstrate real utility KPIs and verified field wins.
